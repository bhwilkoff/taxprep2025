The IRS three-part test for Head of Household

You can file as Head of Household only if ALL three are true:

  1. Unmarried: You were unmarried (or legally separated) on December 31, 2025. ‚úÖ (Your divorce is final)
  2. Paid more than half the cost of your home: You paid more than 50% of the costs to keep up your main home during 2025 (rent/mortgage, utilities, repairs, property tax, food eaten at home, etc.)
  3. Qualifying person lived with you: A qualifying person (such as your child) lived in your home for MORE than half the year ‚Äî that means more than 183 nights from January 1 through December 31, 2025.
    Important: this test uses nights in your home, not legal custody. You can qualify for HOH even as the non-custodial parent if your children actually lived with you more than 183 nights.

Credits vs. deductions
A deduction reduces your taxable income, so its value depends on your tax rate. A credit reduces your tax bill directly. A $1,000 credit saves you $1,000 in taxes; a $1,000 deduction saves you $220 if you're in the 22% bracket.
The Child and Dependent Care Credit (Form 2441) applies if you paid a daycare, babysitter, after-school program, or summer day camp. The child must be a qualifying dependent on your return. Since you're not claiming your children as dependents, this credit only applies if you have other qualifying dependents.
Deductible care expenses max out at $3,000 for one qualifying person or $6,000 for two or more. The credit percentage ranges from 20%‚Äì35% depending on your income.
The American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC) is worth up to $2,500 per student for the first four years of higher education. The Lifetime Learning Credit is worth up to $2,000. Both phase out at higher incomes.

Colorado State Income Tax ‚Äî DR 0104
Colorado has a flat 4.4% income tax rate for 2025, starting from federal taxable income
üèîÔ∏è
How Colorado taxes work
Colorado starts from your federal taxable income (Form 1040, Line 15), then applies Colorado-specific additions and subtractions to arrive at your Colorado taxable income. That amount is multiplied by 4.4% to get your Colorado tax. Colorado withholding from your W-2 (Box 17) is then credited against that amount.
Colorado Subtractions (things that reduce your Colorado tax)
Interest from U.S. savings bonds, Treasury bills, notes, and bonds is exempt from Colorado income tax. If you entered an amount in the "Box 3 ‚Äî U.S. savings bond interest" field earlier, the same amount goes here. This is a subtraction on Colorado Form DR 0104AD, Line 5.
Colorado allows a subtraction for qualifying pension and annuity income. If you received PERA distributions, Social Security, military retirement, or other pension income in 2025, some or all may be subtractable. For individuals under 65, the limit is generally tied to the source of the income. Leave at 0 if not applicable.
Colorado has several other specific subtractions (wildfire mitigation expenses, capital gain subtraction for Colorado-source gains held 5+ years, Native American income, etc.). Leave at 0 if none apply.
Colorado Additions (things that increase your Colorado tax)
Most Colorado filers have no additions. This would include things like interest/dividends from non-Colorado state or local bonds, or Colorado-specific income adjustments. Leave at 0 if none apply.
Colorado Credits
If you made quarterly estimated tax payments to the Colorado Department of Revenue, enter the total here. Enter 0 if you only had withholding from your W-2.
Colorado offers various credits including the Child Care Contribution Credit, Earned Income Tax Credit (25% of federal EITC), Property Tax/Rent/Heat Credit, and others. Leave at 0 if none apply, and note that the EITC typically only applies at lower income levels.
